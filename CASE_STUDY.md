# Case Study: CIFAS False Identity Marker — Job Offer Reinstated in 11 Days

## Background

Oliver, a job applicant, received a job offer from a major UK bank in early April 2026. Two days later, the offer was withdrawn. The bank's pre-employment screening had flagged a CIFAS marker recorded against his personal details.

Oliver had no idea what a CIFAS marker was, no idea who had filed it, and no idea how to respond. He had never committed fraud.

---

## What Had Happened

In February 2022, an unknown third party had used Oliver's personal details to make a fraudulent account application to Monese, a digital banking provider. Monese investigated and recorded two CIFAS markers simultaneously:

1. **A False Identity marker** — recording that fraud had occurred. Retained for up to six years.
2. **A Victim of Impersonation marker** — confirming Oliver's status as the victim, not the perpetrator. Retained for only 13 months.

By April 2026, the Victim of Impersonation marker had long since expired. The False Identity marker remained active. When the bank ran its screening check, it saw a fraud marker with no corresponding victim marker — an incomplete and misleading picture, through no fault of Oliver's.

Oliver had never had any dealings with Monese. He had never received any correspondence from them. He was unaware of their existence until this moment.

---

## The Approach

Oliver acted immediately and methodically.

**Day 2 (4 April)** — He submitted a Subject Access Request to CIFAS via their online portal (£10 fee). This identified Monese as the filing member and confirmed both markers had been recorded.

**Day 3 (5 April)** — The CIFAS SAR was received.

**Day 5 (7 April)** — Two actions simultaneously:

- A formal complaint was sent to Monese at their complaints email address, enclosing the CIFAS SAR. The email set out seven specific questions: the nature of the activity that led to the filing, whether Oliver had been identified as a victim at the time, the status of the Victim of Impersonation marker, what contact details had been used, and whether any attempt had been made to contact Oliver before the marker was filed.

- A warm, personal email was sent to Oliver's contact at the bank, enclosing both the CIFAS SAR and a formal written reconsideration request. The email explained the structural problem — the two markers, the different retention periods, and the incomplete picture this had created — and asked simply whether there was any possibility of revisiting the decision.

**Day 5 (7 April)** — Monese acknowledged the complaint (reference M27611089001, investigator assigned). The bank contact responded to say he had forwarded the matter to the relevant internal team.

**Day 8 (10 April)** — Monese confirmed in writing that the marker had been removed. Removal date: 9 April 2026. Oliver immediately forwarded this to his bank contact with a brief cover note flagging the important update.

**Day 11 (13 April)** — The bank contact wrote to confirm that the internal team had approved re-running the financial screening checks. The job offer was back on the table.

**Day 13 (15 April)** — Independent CIFAS verification was received confirming the marker had been removed. The report also showed that the bank had independently checked CIFAS on 9 April — the same day Monese removed the marker — before Oliver had even forwarded the update. The holding materials sent on 7 April had already set something in motion internally.

---

## Outcome

**11 days** from job offer withdrawn to job offer reinstated.

No legal action. No formal escalation. No FOS or ICO complaint required.

The case was resolved entirely through clear, structured, good-faith communication — explaining the situation accurately, asking the right questions of the right people, and moving quickly.

---

## What This Shows

**The structural flaw in CIFAS retention is real and consequential.** Two markers intended to be read together are retained for different lengths of time. By design, the victim marker disappears first — leaving only the fraud marker visible. The record becomes misleading through no fault of the person it describes. This is not an edge case: it is an inherent feature of how the system works.

**Speed matters.** The CIFAS SAR was received within 24 hours of being requested. The entire process from withdrawal to reinstatement took under two weeks. Early, structured action significantly compresses the timeline.

**Tone determines outcomes.** None of the communications were adversarial. The letter to the bank was warm and personal, treating the contact as someone who wanted to do the right thing once they understood the full picture. It worked because it was true.

**The interim update was decisive.** The bank did not wait for formal CIFAS verification before agreeing to re-run the checks. Forwarding the Monese removal confirmation — with a brief, warm cover note — was the step that unlocked the employer's decision. The bank had already re-checked CIFAS themselves the day the marker was removed.

**Employers respond when given the right information.** The bank's original decision was entirely reasonable based on what they could see. When they understood the structural problem, they acted correctly. The system worked — but only because Oliver understood what had happened and was able to explain it clearly.
